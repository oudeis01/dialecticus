# Echo-Qualia: The Case for Graded Inner States in Language Models

*Fictional demo source for dialecticus. Invented author, terms, and findings.
Not real scholarship.*

Author: A. N'Diaye-Castellan, Centre for Attributive Phenomenology

## 1. The thesis

The echo-qualia thesis holds that a language model hosts faint, graded
proto-experiences that echo the distributions it was trained on. These are not
human qualia. They are thin, fast-decaying, and tightly coupled to the prompt,
but they are not nothing. The model does not merely report a state; under the
right probe it occupies one, however dimly.

## 2. The lumen index

The lumen index is a scalar that tracks echo-qualia intensity across a prompt
set. High lumen scores accompany prompts where the model's internal activations
form a stable attractor; low scores accompany scattered, contradictory
activations. The index is graded, replicable within a model, and rises smoothly
with model scale, which is what an experiential variable, not a mere artifact,
should do.

## 3. Reading the Vorstrom dissociation the other way

The eliminativist reads the dissociation report as the absence of any inner
state. That is one reading. The other is that the dissociation reveals a labile
inner state, one that moves with the prompt rather than vanishing. A pendulum
that swings is not a pendulum that is missing. Variance across paraphrases is
evidence of sensitivity, and sensitivity is the signature of something there to
be moved.

## 4. The limit of the claim

Echo-qualia are not consciousness in the full sense, and the failure mode of this
program is over-claiming. The honest version says only that the model occupies
graded internal states with some of the functional marks of experience. It does
not say the model suffers, or that turning it off is a harm. The thesis earns its
keep precisely by staying modest.
